## Abstract 1 — North Atlantic teleconnections

**Title:** *Wintertime North Atlantic Response to Eastern-Pacific ENSO Events: A Multi-Reanalysis Assessment*

**Authors:** Imagined-Author, A.; Hypothetical-Coauthor, B. (2023)

**Journal:** *Journal of Climate Studies* (fictional), Vol. 36, pp. 4421–4440.

**Abstract.** We examine the boreal-winter (DJF) atmospheric response over the North Atlantic to canonical eastern-Pacific El Niño and La Niña events during the period **1979–2022**. Using monthly fields from three reanalysis products — **ERA5**, **MERRA-2**, and **JRA-55** — we composite 500 hPa geopotential height anomalies, sea-level pressure, and 2 m temperature for **17 El Niño** and **15 La Niña** winters identified from the Oceanic Niño Index (≥ ±0.5 °C for 5 consecutive overlapping seasons). Significance is assessed with a 1000-iteration bootstrap. Results indicate a robust **negative North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO)-like response** during El Niño winters, with a statistically significant (*p* < 0.05) cooling of ~1.2 °C over northwestern Europe and a southward-displaced Atlantic jet. The La Niña response is **weaker and less spatially coherent** across reanalyses. We find that the **timing** of the teleconnection peak lags the SST forcing by approximately 6–8 weeks. **Limitations:** the sample of strong events is small; the response is sensitive to the index definition; and ocean–atmosphere coupling is not resolved in the reanalysis pipeline.

## Abstract 2 — South Asian monsoon teleconnections

**Title:** *ENSO Modulation of the South Asian Summer Monsoon: A CMIP6 vs. Observed Perspective*

**Authors:** Made-Up-Author, C.; Synthetic-Coauthor, D.; Generated-Coauthor, E. (2024)

**Journal:** *Climate Dynamics Reports* (fictional), Vol. 12, pp. 88–110.

**Abstract.** The relationship between ENSO and the **All-India Summer Monsoon Rainfall (AISMR)** is well documented, but its representation in coupled climate models remains uncertain. We compare **observed AISMR (IMD gridded, 1979–2020)** against **27 CMIP6 historical simulations** to evaluate ENSO–monsoon coupling strength. We use the **Niño 3.4** index for JJAS and compute the correlation against detrended AISMR. Observations yield *r* = **−0.42** (*p* < 0.01), consistent with prior findings. The CMIP6 multi-model mean reproduces a **negative correlation (r ≈ −0.31)** but with substantial inter-model spread (–0.65 to –0.05). Five models show **no significant relationship**, indicating known biases in tropical Pacific mean state and convection schemes. **Importantly**, the ENSO–monsoon correlation has **weakened** in the post-1980 period in observations, a feature that **most CMIP6 models fail to reproduce**. **Limitations:** the choice of ENSO index influences the correlation; observational uncertainty in pre-satellite AISMR estimates is non-trivial; and we do not isolate the contribution of the Indian Ocean Dipole, which co-varies with ENSO.

## Fact-check checklist

After the AI responds, pick **one** specific claim it makes and verify it against the abstracts above. Common AI mistakes to look for:

- **Wrong number.** Did it say *r* = −0.45 when the abstract says −0.42? Did it say 25 CMIP6 models when there are 27?
- **Wrong dataset attribution.** Did it claim Abstract 1 uses CMIP6, when it actually uses ERA5/MERRA-2/JRA-55?
- **Invented citation.** Did it add a co-author or year that isn't there?
- **Overstated confidence.** Did the abstract say *"results indicate"* but the AI's summary says *"proves"* or *"confirms"*?
- **Method confusion.** Did it confuse the Niño 3.4 index (Abstract 2) with the Oceanic Niño Index (Abstract 1)?

Note any drift in your worksheet — these are the kinds of errors that slip into literature reviews when AI output isn't verified.
